Japanese garden art is not merely a collection of horticultural techniques, but a complex aesthetic, philosophical, and sacred system that seeks to capture the ideal forms of nature. For the beginning garden builder, the first and most important realization is that the Japanese garden is a kind of "three-dimensional painting," where the designer uses plants, rocks, and open spaces the way an artist uses colors on a palette. In this article, I will introduce the constituent elements of the Japanese garden, their deeper cultural symbolism, and the plant palette suited to Hungary's continental climate.

### The Philosophical and Historical Foundations of Japanese Garden Architecture

The roots of Japanese garden culture can be traced to the island nation's indigenous religion, Shinto, as well as to Buddhism and Taoism arriving from continental Asia. According to the animistic worldview of Shinto, every element of nature — unusually shaped rocks, ancient trees, and waterfalls — is the dwelling place of deities (kami). The garden (niwa) originally meant a purified, gravel-covered sacred space prepared to receive the gods.

Buddhism, particularly its Zen branch, elevated garden-making into an instrument of meditation and self-knowledge. During the Muromachi period (1338–1573), the karesansui — the dry rock garden — became dominant, representing the universe at the highest level of abstraction, without water, using only stones and sand. Parallel to the development of the tea ceremony, the chaniwa (tea garden) emerged as the setting for inner tranquility and ritual purity.

#### The Shift in Thinking

Harmony over dominion. While the Western, formal approach to gardening emphasizes humanity's mastery over nature, the Japanese garden aims to capture the essence and ideal forms of nature. Its hallmarks are asymmetry, organic forms, and respect for the invisible order of the natural world. The Japanese garden is an essential mirror of the universe. Rocks condense mountains, ponds condense oceans into a single space. It is an idealized, disciplined presentation of nature's most expressive moments, free from unnecessary physical noise.

### Fundamental Aesthetic Categories

When designing a Japanese garden, four key concepts define the spatial experience:

- Ma (Pause, negative space): Empty space does not represent an absence, but an active compositional element that allows the eye to rest and draws attention to focal objects.
- Wabi-Sabi: The aesthetic value found in imperfection, transience, and the beauty of patina wrought by time (such as moss settling on stones).
- Fukinsei (Asymmetry): The search for natural balance by avoiding artificial symmetry. The Japanese gardener never places an even number of elements or a mirror-image arrangement.
- Shizen (Naturalness): The concealment of human intervention. The goal is for the garden to feel as though the landscape formed itself, rather than having been born at a drawing board.

| Garden Style | Name | Primary Function | Characteristic Elements |
|----------|--------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Karesansui | Dry garden | Meditation, contemplation | Raked sand, rocks |
| Tsukiyama | Hill garden | Scenery, strolling | Artificial hills, ponds, bridges |
| Chaniwa | Tea garden | Preparation for the tea ceremony | Stepping stones, stone basin, lantern |
| Tsuboniwa | Courtyard garden | Decoration of small spaces | Minimal planting, ornamental stones |
| Kaiyushiki | Stroll garden | Social life, relaxation | Winding paths, shifting viewpoints   |

### The Structural Elements of the Garden: The Symbolism of Rocks and Stone

Stones form the "skeleton" of the Japanese garden; they radiate stability, strength, and eternity. Classical texts, such as the 11th-century Sakuteiki, refer to the process of garden-making as "setting stones" (ishi wo taten koto). The selection and placement of stones (ishigumi) carries deep spiritual meaning.

### The Art and Rules of Ishigumi

Stones are not scattered randomly, but arranged in a strict hierarchical order. The most important arrangement is the sanzon ishigumi, or Buddhist trinity: a central, tall, vertical stone represents the deity (or heaven), while two smaller stones support it from the sides, symbolizing humanity and the earth.

By stone type, we distinguish:

- Tall vertical stones: Symbolize mountain peaks or energy striving toward the sky.
- Low vertical stones: Provide stability to the composition.
- Lying or horizontal stones: Represent islands, the earth, or tranquility.
- Curved or arched stones: Bring movement and dynamism to the scene.

The beginning garden builder should avoid so-called "taboo stones." These include "sick stones" (with a distorted upper portion), "dead stones" (vertical stones set in an unnatural direction), and "beggar stones" (those with no visual connection to the other elements). Stones must be set into the ground so that at least one third of them lies below the surface, creating the impression that the rock is emerging from the depths and has been there since time immemorial.

### Stepping Stones and Paths (Tobi-ishi)

Paths in the Japanese garden symbolize the journey of life. The arrangement of stepping stones (tobi-ishi) determines the visitor's pace and the direction of their attention. Where the stones are larger and flatter, the gardener invites the viewer to pause and take in the panorama. Paths are rarely straight; the winding course serves both to keep evil spirits at bay (who, according to folklore, can only travel in straight lines) and to expand the perceived space.

### The Role and Symbolic Representation of Water

Water symbolizes purity, the cycle of life, and renewal. In an authentic Japanese garden, water must flow, and its sound drowns out urban noise, fostering inner contemplation.

#### Types of Water Features

- Ponds and islands: The shape of ponds is always asymmetric. They frequently contain an island symbolizing the mythical Mount Horai, the dwelling place of the immortals.
- Waterfalls: The Sakuteiki describes ten different waterfall arrangements. It is important that the waterfall ideally face the moon, so that the nocturnal light may be reflected in the cascade.
- Karesansui (Dry water): In gardens where real water is not feasible, white sand or fine gravel is used to imitate water. Raked lines represent the waves of the sea or the flow of rivers.
- Shishi-odoshi (Deer scarer): This bamboo device was originally used to keep animals away, but its rhythmic clacking has become one of the garden's most distinctive meditative sounds.

### Plant Selection and Seasonality

In the Japanese garden, plants are chosen not for the color of their flowers, but for their texture, form, and their response to the changing seasons. The goal is to create a "filtered reality," where the plants represent the essence of forests and mountains.

Dominant Species and Their Meanings

- Pine (Matsu): The evergreen pine is a symbol of longevity and steadfastness. It is often pruned into a "cloud form" (niwaki) to evoke the image of an aged, weathered tree.
- Japanese maple (Momiji): With its dramatic autumn foliage colors, it serves as a reminder of the transience of life.
- Bamboo: Its flexibility and rapid growth make it a symbol of purity and uprightness. It also functions as a fence or windbreak.
- Moss: Evokes a sense of tranquility and ancient origins. The Japanese regard moss not as a weed, but as a valuable ground cover.
- Cherry tree (Sakura): The spring blossoming is a symbol of renewal and ephemeral beauty.

### Adapting to the Hungarian Climate

The beginning garden builder in Hungary must take into account that the Japanese climate is more humid and more uniform than the domestic continental climate. Substituting certain species or providing specialized care is essential for success.

| Japanese Species | Hungarian Alternative | Notes |
|----------|--------------|--------------------|
| Azalea | Rhododendron hybrids | Requires acidic soil (ericaceous compost) |
| Moss | Asarum europaeum, Cushion moss | Viable in shaded spots with regular watering |
| Bamboo | Fargesia species (non-invasive) | Root barrier is mandatory for other species |
| Japanese maple | Katsura or Beni-otake cultivars | Plant in partial shade, sheltered from wind |
| Yew | Taxus baccata | Excellent for shaping as niwaki |


### Cultivating Trained Trees in the Bonsai Style

When cultivating bonsai and trained garden trees (niwaki), the goal is for the tree to reflect the passage of time and its struggle with the elements of nature.

Chokkan (Formal upright): A straight trunk that conveys the dignity of the tallest tree in the forest.
Shakan (Slanting trunk): Imitates a tree that leans toward the wind or light.
Kengai (Cascade): The trunk bends below the rim of the pot, as though hanging over a sheer cliff face.

### Closing Thoughts

There is something extraordinary, an inexplicable harmony in such a garden that fills the visitor with a singular feeling when they step into a well-composed Japanese garden. Whether someone builds the garden at home from its characteristic elements, that same sense of calm and continuous growth accompanies the experience.

It is important to understand: a Japanese garden is never finished, just like a bonsai. Its design and construction are merely the first step in a lifelong process.

Time, as a designer, helps us make it into an authentic and meaningful garden; the spreading of moss, the patina on the stones (sabi), and the aging of the trees are what truly give the space its value. Our garden demands constant care, which cannot be an obligation, but rather the very purpose of existence. Raking the gravel and sweeping the fallen leaves is a daily practice of self-knowledge — the perfect expression of humility and our connection to nature.

The more you look, the more you see, and the more you find within yourself.
